Avoid Inline Labels #683

Issue: When the label disappears as the user starts typing, errors become difficult to correct and users sometimes forget what they are supposed to enter.

Advice: Avoid inline labels (though they may be acceptable for forms of 1 or 2 fields), and be cautious when implementing floating labels (in particular, ensure the error message state performs well).

Issue first observed Sep 2016. Guideline updated 6 times since, most recently Feb 2023.

Field Labels & Microcopy

Avoid Inline Labels #683

Mark as Read

Importance:Essential

Platforms:Desktop WebMobile WebMobile App

Distinctions:Web ConventionLow Cost

Research Findings

“It’s quite annoying that it [the label] disappears when I click ‘Next’ because I don’t know what to enter”, a participant observed when she tried to fill in a field whose inline label vanished upon her activation of the field.​‌‌‌‍‌‌‌‌‌‌‍‌‌‌‍‍‌‌‌‌‌‌‎

Core UX Issue

Inline labels, seen here at Overstock, disappear as users enter the field or begin typing — removing vital information that users rely on when filling out form fields.

“Inline labels” are field labels that are placed inside the form field and that disappear when the field is activated or a user begins typing. While this design saves space, this approach has serious drawbacks and caused great difficulty for some test participants in Baymard’s large-scale usability testing — both on desktop and mobile sites. The severe flow and typing issues caused by inline labels often results in users having to delete their entire input just to see the label again. In a few instances during testing, such inline labels were the direct cause for site abandonment.

It’s worth noting that most desktop sites seem to have “gotten the message” with regards to inline labels: only 2% of sites in our desktop benchmark of the top-60 sites use inline labels, which is down from 7% in 2016. On mobile, the violation rate is higher, with 36% of sites using inline labels. Yet on mobile the issue is likely to affect even more users than on desktop, as the smaller viewport makes it even more difficult to establish context when filling out forms (especially if doing so in landscape mode).

At Walmart, during a previous round of testing, as a test participant tapped the “Next” button to progress to the next field, the label disappeared as the field was selected (second image). The participant had to go on to the next field to see what she was supposed to enter, then return to the field she had skipped.

This participant during a previous round of testing submitted several different spellings of “Houston” when trying to use Fandango to find theaters in the area. Because the label disappeared, she did not realize the site also required either a state name or zip code, and she eventually abandoned the site, as she (erroneously but understandably) concluded the message meant that Fandango did not support theaters in Houston. Had the field label been visible at all times, abandonment might have been prevented.

View 3 Desktop Web, 9 Mobile Web, and 6 Mobile App examples

The reason that inline labels are so problematic is that, without permanently visible labels, form fields are just indistinguishable rectangular boxes in the interface. What distinguishes one field from the next is its label — the label is the defining context for that box. When labels disappear — as they do with inline labels — suddenly the only information provided for the field is the user’s own input. This not only makes it more difficult for users to fill out the fields, but it also makes it much more difficult to correct any validation errors they have.

Besides making errors more difficult to correct, it was also observed that test participants were often simply doubtful of what they were supposed to type while they were filling out the field. For example, if the user suddenly gets distracted by a visual element on the page (e.g., a live chat — see #940: Avoid Non-User-Initiated Live Chat Dialogs and on Mobile be Cautious with ‘Sticky’ Chat Elements), they might be unsure about what they were supposed to type. Those users who forget or become in doubt are at a loss, and they have to delete the data they’ve already entered just to figure out what the context of the field is. This adds significant friction to the typing process.

Account sign-in is one instance where inline labels provide sufficient context.

However, there are a few instances where inline labels do have merit. When there are only 1–2 fields (for example a search field or perhaps a sign-in form) the user will rarely forget the context.

Yet as soon as there are more fields, different types of data are needed, or the fields are used infrequently inline labels again become problematic. Also, if there are requirements for the field input, inline labels are typically a poor choice even if it is only a single field (as the earlier Fandango “Houston” example illustrates).

It should be underscored that inline placeholder text in and of itself isn’t bad (in fact, it’s a great feature) — it just shouldn’t be used for the primary label. Instead, inline placeholder text is perfect for brief descriptions that may help frame or further clarify the field’s context without being necessary to understand it (see #349: Encourage 'Thematic' and 'Symptom' Searches with Placeholder Text and #658: Use Localized Input Masks for Restricted Inputs).

Core UX Solution

To avoid issues caused by inline labels, simply “sticking with the basics” and providing labels outside form fields is best, as seen here at Argos.

Labels outside the form field also works best for mobile interfaces. There’s much less of a chance users will lose context when typing in their information.

We’ve identified another 202 examples of ‘Form labels are placed outside the field,’ illustrating this implementation type across 114 case studies and 18 different industries.

View 106 Desktop Web, 83 Mobile Web, and 13 Mobile App examples

The best-performing design for alleviating these issues is not to use inline labels and simply to use traditional static text form field labels placed outside the field. (And above the field — see #685.)

In a Floating Label design, the label sits inside the field but then floats to the top of the field when the user begins typing.

We’ve identified another 142 examples of ‘The site uses floating labels,’ illustrating this implementation type across 80 case studies and 15 different industries.

View 71 Desktop Web, 64 Mobile Web, and 7 Mobile App examples

Additionally, “Floating Labels”, where the label sits inside the field but then floats to the top of the field when the user begins typing, is an increasingly popular alternative to inline labels — 22% of sites in the desktop benchmark use floating labels, up from 5% in 2016. Floating Labels should resolve the context issues of inline labels and the pattern thus shows promise. It’s important to note that we have yet to verify this design pattern through large-scale testing, however, and there could be issues with the floating label implementation (see also this article).

If experimenting with floating labels or similar designs, it is recommended to pay special attention to the form validation error experience, since it has been noted that sites with Floating Labels haven’t implemented the validation error state very well. Typically, these sites only allow room for very short or no error messages, which is highly problematic (see #722: Use ‘Adaptive Error Messages’ that Match the User’s Specific Subissue). Therefore, in addition to maximizing the label size, and checking code for accessibility issues, care should be taken in implementing the error message state if deciding to pursue a floating label design.

Guideline: Avoid inline labels (though they may be acceptable for forms of 1 or 2 fields), and be cautious when implementing floating labels (in particular, ensure the error message state performs well).
