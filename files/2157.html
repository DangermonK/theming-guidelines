“Added to Cart” Behavior & Confirmation

Persist Cart Contents and Inform Users of any Changes to Cart Items #2157

Issue: Users often add items to their cart with the intention to return at a later time, and they will miss out on potential purchases if items disappear from their cart unexpectedly.

Advice: Persist cart contents across user sessions regardless of account or sign-in status — ideally indefinitely. Otherwise, communicate how long cart items will remain. Additionally, inform users of any changes to cart items, and provide a way for mobile users to send or share the cart.

Issue first observed Sep 2016. Guideline updated 6 times since, most recently Sep 2022.

Importance:Impactful

Platforms:Desktop WebMobile WebMobile App

Research Findings

Across multiple rounds of usability studies, we’ve consistently observed participants effectively utilizing the shopping cart as a way to “keep tabs” on items they are considering, using the cart as a “Save” or “Wishlist” feature (see #798: How to Implement 'Save' Features). In practice, users may add items to their cart with the desire to purchase them at a later time — days, weeks, or even months later. If users return to the site later to find the items saved in their cart have vanished, they may have to spend valuable time and effort relocating and readding those items to the cart for purchase. Worse still, they may be unable to refind an item or decide it’s not worth the effort, resulting in a preventable missed sale.​‌‌‌‍‌‌‌‌‌‌‍‌‌‌‍‍‌‌‌‌‌‌‎

Core User Behavior Observed

“I think that there is no harm just throwing stuff in my basket to keep tabs on things I was interested in, then I just sort of go through my basket and make choices later…I might just add this guy to my basket to keep tabs on it.” This participant on Sephora added items she was considering to her cart even though she wasn’t fully done with her evaluation, intending to reconsider them at a later time to make her purchase decision. Using the cart as a kind of storage tool is a common way for users to keep track of items they are still deciding on.

“Yeah, I think if I bought it in the set I’d only be saving $20, so that’s not too bad at all.” This participant on Great Jones added products to her cart to compare the savings of purchasing them as a set (offered elsewhere on the site). In practice, users may use the cart as a storage and comparison tool, comparing items in the cart to each other or to items on other sites.

“I’m just going to browse and add the ones that I like to the cart so that I can compare later.” This participant shopping on Staples added products she liked to the cart while in the product list and later used the cart to compare the items.

“Sometimes as I’m shopping, I’ll add a couple of things, but later I’ll choose between one or the other or something.” Another participant on Theo Chocolates explained how she often decides among alternative products after adding them to the cart first.

“I like these, maybe I’ll put them in the cart to save them for now.” Another participant on Greats added shoes he was interested in to the cart to save them while he continued browsing.

3 additional examples

“We’re signed in to my profile so there are a lot of items in my cart. It’s a way to save items. Like, this looks interesting, but I’m not going to buy it right now — throw it in the cart.” Another participant from a previous round of testing on Amazon explained why he had a message saying “15 Items in your Basket have changed price” — he used the cart to save many items for later consideration.

“I add the two or three that I like, and then I go to the cart and see which one I like the best”, a test participant said when looking for sleeping bags that met a certain temperature rating. In testing, multiple participants displayed similar behavior when the products were more spec-driven, moving back-and-forth from product lists to product pages.

“I usually add items that I find interesting. Then I kinda have them stored in my cart. Then you can…later on you can go in there”, a test participant on H&M explained. She casually added products she found interesting as she browsed the site (first image) to evaluate them in the cart later on (second image).

Adding items to the cart as a way to save them for later has been a commonly observed behavior across Baymard’s multiple usability studies. For instance, in our most recent large-scale mobile study, 22% of test participants used the cart as an unofficial “Save” feature (compared with only 7% who used designated, site-provided “Save” features).

Our quantitative study shows that 42% of respondents rely on the cart to save products.

To further investigate users’ preferences for saving items, we conducted a quantitative study of 3,740 US internet shoppers. Interestingly, using the cart as the primary way to save items was the most popular option at 42%, with explicit “Save” or wishlist features coming in second place (30%). After adding items to the cart, users may opt to use a cart “Save” feature (see #622) in order to proceed with checkout for only a selection of the items they have added to the cart, but some users may leave their entire cart with the intention to return and repurchase at a later time. Effectively, for some users what appears to be cart abandonment is in fact only a delay in the eventual purchase.

In practice, users expect to be able to return to their cart and review all of their previously added items, potentially to compare and reconsider their items or to eventually resume their purchase process. However, if those items are no longer there, they will be unable to pick up where they left off. Indeed, some users are likely to fear a lack of cart persistence, worrying the time and effort they spend exploring items would need to be repeated in order to relocate the items in their cart. In effect, if cart items are not persisted, a “soft abandonment” is more likely to become a “hard abandonment” as users give up on purchasing items that were removed.

Core UX Solution

Users generally expect previously added items to persist indefinitely, even if they do not have an account or are not signed in. However, signed in users will have the benefit of carts that persist across not only multiple sessions, but multiple devices, as demonstrated by Everlane on desktop (first image) and mobile.

For the cart to function as an effective storage tool, items added should persist across multiple browsing sessions, allowing users the opportunity to return and pick up where they left off. Ideally, cart items should persist indefinitely or until the items become out of stock or unavailable, at which point users returning to the cart should be notified and presented with quality alternatives (see #820). As with cart “Save” features, users generally expect to be able to return to their cart at a later time to find their previously added items regardless of account or sign-in status. (However, signed-in users can benefit from persistent carts across devices, being able to sign in to another computer or mobile device to see items from a previous browsing session, and it can be helpful to communicate this to users in the cart — see implementation detail #1 below.)

2 Implementation Details

In addition to persisting cart contents, it’s furthermore sometimes important to communicate to users how long items will remain in the cart, as well if there have been any changes to the items since they last visited.

1) Communicate to Guest Users How Long Cart Items Will Remain in the Cart

L.L.Bean offers a description of the cart persistence to guest users — informing them of how long items will be stored in the cart. Additionally, users are gently nudged to create an account (or sign in) if they’d like to save their cart items for a longer period of time.

In practice saving the cart indefinitely is not always an option, and in these cases users need to be informed of how long their items will remain. Otherwise, users will have no way of knowing that the cart does not persist indefinitely and may be surprised to return at a later time to see their cart has reset. Having a single line describing how long the cart content will remain helps set users’ expectations and can help put them at ease. For a more detailed explanation, this message can also be presented within a tooltip.

Gap’s cart alerts users that items in their cart will only be saved for a short time, helping set expectations for if they leave their cart and later return.

On Asos, users are informed that items are only guaranteed for 60 minutes.

For extremely limited items that are sure to sell out, a countdown timer gives users the most precise expectation for the finite cart persistence, as shown here on StubHub.

Additionally, even when cart items persist across multiple sessions, high demand or limited stock items risk selling out before users return. In these cases, the cart should also alert users so that they can take this important information into account when making their purchase decision. For extremely limited items that are sure to sell out, sites can also consider a countdown timer as a reminder of the much-restricted cart persistence window.

2) Inform Users of any Changes to Cart Items

On Amazon, returning users are reminded when items they have stored in the cart change in price or availability.

When items stored in the cart do change in status — becoming out of stock or changing in price — users should be alerted to this fact with a simple notification at the top of the cart. In practice, bringing attention to details like “low inventory” or “now on clearance” can help motivate users to finally continue to checkout, while communicating price changes shows a site is attempting to be transparent when it comes to items that have been added to the cart.

Mobile and App Considerations

Build offers an “Email Cart” button, allowing users to save their cart for their own later use or to share it with someone else.

During mobile testing, many participants mentioned they like to browse for products on their phone, but then actually complete the checkout later on their laptop or desktop device, as going through multiple checkout steps and filling out form fields is tedious on mobile. This behavior of finding a product on mobile, but completing a checkout later on desktop, can be supported simply by having a feature for sending or saving the cart content to an email address for easy access later on a nonmobile device. Of course, if the user is signed in to an account, any cart content should sync between mobile and desktop devices, as detailed above.

While being able to send or share a cart on desktop is “nice to have”, we observed far fewer participants making use of this feature during desktop testing, whereas several participants on mobile did. Thus, while it’s not “wrong” to provide this feature on desktop, it’s likely to receive much less use than the same feature on the mobile site.

Guideline: Persist cart contents across user sessions regardless of account or sign-in status — ideally indefinitely. Otherwise, communicate how long cart items will remain. Additionally, inform users of any changes to cart items, and provide a way for mobile users to send or share the cart.
